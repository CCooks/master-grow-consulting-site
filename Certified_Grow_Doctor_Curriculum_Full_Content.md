# Certified Grow Doctor Curriculum – Full Modules & Assessments

This document expands upon the Grow Doctor Certification Program by providing comprehensive instructional content and assessment guidance for each module.  The curriculum is designed to prepare candidates to serve both home growers and licensed cultivation facilities.  It incorporates Nevada’s legal requirements for home cultivation and licensed establishments, ensuring graduates meet state compliance standards【347000102993283†L349-L364】【98296420791439†L268-L277】.

---

## Module 1 – Cannabis Botany & Plant Physiology

### Learning objectives

- Explain the taxonomy and diversity of cannabis species (Cannabis sativa, Cannabis indica, hybrids and ruderalis).
- Identify the major anatomical structures of the cannabis plant and describe their functions.
- Describe how photosynthesis, respiration and transpiration drive plant growth.
- Understand the role of plant hormones (auxins, cytokinins, gibberellins, ethylene, abscisic acid) in regulating development.
- Explain sexual reproduction, pollination, seed formation and cloning.
- Understand the biosynthesis of cannabinoids (THC, CBD, minor cannabinoids) and terpenes in trichomes.

### Instructional content

1. **Species & cultivars:**
   - Differences in morphology between *Cannabis sativa* (taller, narrower leaves, longer flowering period) and *Cannabis indica* (shorter stature, broad leaves, shorter flowering) as well as modern hybrids.
   - Autoflowering (ruderalis) genetics – benefits (short lifecycle, independence from photoperiod) and limitations (smaller yields, lower potency).
   - Chemotypes: high‐THC, balanced THC/CBD, and high‐CBD cultivars; how genetics influence cannabinoid profiles.

2. **Anatomy:**
   - **Roots:** anchor the plant and absorb water and nutrients; types of root systems; importance of healthy root zone and oxygen.
   - **Stem & branches:** structural support and transport of water, nutrients and photosynthates; node vs internode; importance of stem diameter.
   - **Leaves:** fan leaves (photosynthesis and transpiration), sugar leaves (photosynthesis, trichomes), stipules.
   - **Flowers (buds):** calyxes, pistils, trichomes; difference between male and female flowers; significance of resin gland structure (stalked and sessile trichomes).

3. **Physiology:**
   - **Photosynthesis:** light energy conversion; importance of blue/red spectrum; C3 photosynthetic pathway; role of chloroplasts; effect of light intensity on growth and yield.
   - **Respiration & transpiration:** energy production and water movement; how stomata regulate gas exchange; environmental factors affecting transpiration.
   - **Photoperiodism:** long-day (vegetative) vs short-day (flowering) response; how 12/12 triggers flowering; differences with autoflower cultivars.
   - **Plant hormones:** synthesis and roles of auxins (apical dominance), cytokinins (cell division), gibberellins (stem elongation), ethylene (senescence and stress responses) and abscisic acid (drought tolerance).  Discuss practical uses such as cloning hormone gels and gibberellic acid sprays for reversing sex.

4. **Genetics & reproduction:**
   - Dioecious nature of cannabis; male vs female identification; hermaphroditism (causes such as stress or light leaks).
   - Pollination and seed development; differences between open pollination, controlled breeding and feminized seed production.
   - Vegetative propagation: cloning procedures (cutting selection, hormone use, humidity domes) and tissue culture basics (meristem culture, micropropagation).

5. **Secondary metabolites:**
   - Cannabinoid biosynthesis pathways; precursor molecules (CBG) and enzyme‐mediated conversion to THC, CBD, CBC etc.; role of heat and decarboxylation.
   - Terpene synthesis; major terpene classes (monoterpenes, sesquiterpenes) and their influence on aroma and therapeutic effects.

### Assessments

- **Quiz:** Ten multiple‐choice questions covering plant anatomy, physiology and reproductive biology.
- **Diagram assignment:** Students draw and label a cannabis plant, including root, stem, leaves, flowers and trichomes, and explain the function of each part.
- **Short essay:** Compare and contrast *C. sativa* and *C. indica* morphologies and discuss how hybrids combine traits.
- **Research report:** Investigate how photoperiod influences cannabinoid production and present findings (two pages).  Include at least one peer‐reviewed reference.
- **Knowledge check:** Identify the stages of the plant lifecycle (germination, seedling, vegetative, flowering, ripening) and describe important considerations at each stage.

## Module 2 – Environmental Control & Lighting

### Learning objectives

- Maintain optimal temperature, humidity, and airflow for cannabis growth in both small tents and large cultivation facilities.
- Select appropriate lighting technologies and design a lighting layout based on plant density and canopy geometry.
- Interpret and calculate Vapour Pressure Deficit (VPD) to fine‑tune environmental conditions.
- Understand carbon dioxide (CO₂) supplementation and safe handling procedures.
- Design scalable environmental control systems for commercial facilities.

### Instructional content

1. **Temperature & humidity management:**
   - Optimal temperature ranges: 70–85 °F (21–29 °C) during vegetative phase; 65–80 °F (18–27 °C) during flowering; lower nighttime temperatures can improve colour and terpene production.
   - Relative humidity (RH) guidelines: 65 % for seedlings; 55 % for vegetative plants; 45 % for flowering; 35 % during late flower to prevent bud rot.  Introduce VPD charts and how to calculate VPD using temperature and humidity to ensure proper transpiration rates.
   - Strategies for controlling humidity: humidifiers, dehumidifiers, proper ventilation, and water content in growing medium.

2. **Airflow & ventilation:**
   - Importance of fresh air exchange; negative pressure to prevent odour leaks; sizing exhaust fans based on room volume (CFM calculations); use of oscillating fans to prevent microclimates.
   - Carbon filters for odour control and to meet legal requirements for enclosed grows【347000102993283†L349-L364】.
   - Intake filters to prevent pest and pathogen entry; use of HEPA filtration in large facilities.

3. **Lighting technologies:**
   - **Fluorescent (T5):** Good for seedlings and clones; low heat but limited intensity.
   - **High‐Intensity Discharge (HID):** Metal halide (MH) for veg, high‐pressure sodium (HPS) for flower; high heat output; good penetration but less efficient than LEDs.
   - **LED:** Full‐spectrum diodes with high efficiency; adjustable spectrum; lower heat; higher upfront cost but energy savings; different form factors (board, bar, COB).  Compare PPFD outputs and canopy penetration.
   - Determine light intensity targets (500–700 µmol/m²/s for veg; 700–900 µmol/m²/s for flower); discuss Daily Light Integral (DLI) and appropriate photoperiod scheduling (18/6 for veg; 12/12 for flower; 20/4 for autoflowers).

4. **CO₂ supplementation:**
   - Role of CO₂ in photosynthesis; ambient levels (~400 ppm); benefits of supplementation (1,200–1,500 ppm) for increased growth when light, temperature and nutrients are optimal.
   - Delivery systems: CO₂ tanks with regulators, CO₂ generators (burners), and exhale bags; monitoring with CO₂ sensors; safety considerations (risk of asphyxiation; maintain at least one oxygen sensor and alarm).  Only recommended for sealed rooms with adequate lighting and controlled environment.

5. **Environmental monitoring & automation:**
   - Use of thermohygrometers, data loggers and Bluetooth sensors to track conditions; importance of continuous monitoring.
   - Environmental controllers to regulate fans, heaters, dehumidifiers and CO₂; integration with mobile apps; alert systems for parameter deviations.

6. **Facility design & scalability:**
   - Designing environmental systems for commercial grows: proper sizing of HVAC units; understanding latent vs sensible loads; using dehumidification and cooling systems to handle transpiration from thousands of plants.
   - Vertical farming and multi‐tier cultivation: improved space efficiency but requires careful light distribution and airflow design; structural considerations; walkway spacing and mechanical lifts.
   - Redundancy and risk management: backup generators; secondary dehumidifiers; environmental alarms; preventing catastrophic failures.
   - Energy efficiency: high‐efficiency LEDs, variable‐speed drives for fans, energy recovery systems; evaluating power consumption and ROI.

### Assessments

- **Calculation exercise:** Given temperature and RH data, calculate the VPD and determine whether adjustments are needed.
- **Design project:** Draft an environmental control plan for a 4 × 8 ft tent, specifying exhaust fan size, intake setup, lighting type, and humidity management.
- **Case study:** Analyze a scenario where plants show heat stress; propose solutions including lighting adjustments and HVAC upgrades.
- **Facility plan:** Create a basic environmental design for a 10,000 sq ft cultivation facility, outlining HVAC tonnage, dehumidification capacity, air exchange rates and redundancy.
- **Short answer quiz:** Explain the differences between HID and LED lighting; describe safe CO₂ supplementation procedures; and list key sensors needed for monitoring.

## Module 3 – Nutrients & Water Management

### Learning objectives

- Identify macro- and micronutrient functions and diagnose common deficiencies and toxicities.
- Develop nutrient schedules for soil, coco and hydroponic systems across the plant lifecycle.
- Manage water quality by monitoring pH, electrical conductivity (EC) and total dissolved solids (TDS).
- Design irrigation systems appropriate for small grows and large facilities.

### Instructional content

1. **Essential nutrients:**
   - **Macronutrients:** Nitrogen (leaf growth), phosphorus (root and flower development), potassium (overall health and disease resistance).  Secondary macronutrients include calcium (cell walls), magnesium (chlorophyll synthesis) and sulfur (amino acids).
   - **Micronutrients:** Iron, manganese, zinc, boron, copper, molybdenum and chlorine; required in trace amounts but crucial for enzyme functions.  Discuss deficiency symptoms (chlorosis, necrosis, leaf deformations) and toxicity signs.

2. **Growing media comparison:**
   - **Soil:** Natural and amended soils; living soil ecosystems; water retention and buffering; slower nutrient delivery; organic vs synthetic amendments.
   - **Coco coir:** Inert, high cation exchange capacity; requires precise feeding; benefits of coco/perlite blends.
   - **Hydroponics:** Deep Water Culture (DWC), ebb-and-flow, nutrient film technique (NFT), aeroponics.  Benefits of rapid growth and nutrient uptake; risk of system failures and root diseases; requirement for constant monitoring.

3. **Water quality management:**
   - **pH:** Ideal range of 5.8–6.2 for hydro/coco; 6.2–6.8 for soil.  Explain how pH affects nutrient availability.  Teach how to use pH meters and adjust using acids (phosphoric acid, nitric acid) or bases (potassium hydroxide).
   - **EC/TDS:** Measurement of dissolved salts; importance of matching EC to plant stage (e.g., 0.8–1.2 mS/cm for seedlings, up to 2.5 mS/cm for heavy-feeding flower).  Use of EC meters and calibrating regularly.
   - **Water sources:** Tap water, reverse osmosis (RO), rainwater; pros/cons; necessity of adding calcium/magnesium to RO water.

4. **Nutrient solution mixing:**
   - Step-by-step process: fill reservoir with water, measure initial EC and pH, add base nutrients, measure, add supplements (Cal/Mag, PK boosters), adjust pH last; maintain reservoir temperature (65–70 °F).
   - Importance of not mixing concentrates together before dilution; storing nutrients properly (cool, dark place).

5. **Feeding schedules & deficiency troubleshooting:**
   - Sample feeding schedule for veg (high nitrogen) and flower (higher phosphorus and potassium, lower nitrogen).
   - Recognising and treating nutrient deficiencies (e.g., nitrogen deficiency causes yellowing of lower leaves; magnesium deficiency results in interveinal chlorosis) and toxicities (nutrient burn, leaf tip necrosis).
   - Flush protocols to correct overfeeding (using pH’ed water to remove salts from medium).

6. **Irrigation systems:**
   - **Hand watering:** Suitable for small grows; emphasis on watering to runoff; importance of consistent scheduling.
   - **Drip irrigation:** Components (reservoir, pump, drip lines, emitters); automation with timers and moisture sensors; advantages for large grows and water conservation.
   - **Ebb‑and‑flow / flood tables:** Flood cycles; draining and aeration; risk of root diseases if not properly sterilised.
   - **Automated nutrient dosing:** Peristaltic pumps and dosing controllers; benefits of maintaining consistent EC and pH levels in commercial facilities.

7. **Foliar feeding:**
   - Benefits of delivering micronutrients and beneficial microbes directly to leaves; best practices (lights off; fine mist; only when deficiency present); caution against foliar feeding during bloom to prevent mould.

### Assessments

- **Nutrient schedule assignment:** Create a detailed feeding schedule for a six-week vegetative and eight-week flowering cycle using a provided nutrient line; justify each additive.
- **Diagnostic quiz:** Identify nutrient deficiencies or toxicities from leaf photographs; propose corrective measures.
- **Lab exercise:** Mix a nutrient solution to specific EC and pH targets; measure initial and final values.
- **System design project:** Design an automated drip irrigation system for a 12-plant setup, including pump size, reservoir volume and timer settings.

## Module 4 – Pest & Disease Management

### Learning objectives

- Recognise symptoms of common pests and diseases affecting cannabis crops.
- Develop integrated pest management (IPM) strategies emphasising prevention, monitoring and environmentally responsible control.
- Select and use pesticides or biological agents in compliance with Nevada regulations and safety guidelines.
- Implement record‐keeping and monitoring systems to support IPM and regulatory compliance.

### Instructional content

1. **Identifying pests & diseases:**
   - **Insect pests:** Description, life cycle and damage symptoms of spider mites, two-spotted mites, thrips, fungus gnats, aphids, whiteflies, root aphids, broad mites, russet mites and caterpillars.
   - **Pathogens:** Powdery mildew, downy mildew, botrytis (bud rot), Fusarium, Pythium (root rot), Verticillium wilt, Septoria, viruses (tobacco mosaic virus, hop latent viroid).  Explain conditions favouring disease and typical symptoms.

2. **Prevention & cultural controls:**
   - Sanitation protocols: cleaning grow rooms between cycles, disinfecting tools, wearing clean clothing and gloves.
   - Quarantine procedures for new clones/seeds; using tissue culture or reputable sources to prevent disease introduction.
   - Environmental control: maintaining appropriate VPD to prevent fungal outbreaks; avoiding stagnant air; controlling relative humidity and leaf surface moisture.
   - Crop spacing and pruning to improve airflow and reduce microclimates; removing plant debris promptly.

3. **Monitoring & thresholds:**
   - Scouting routines: weekly visual inspections; use of magnification (10×–60× microscopes) to detect early pests; sticky cards for monitoring flying insects; monitoring root zone for fungus gnats.
   - Establishing thresholds: determining when intervention is necessary based on pest population density and plant age.
   - Keeping detailed pest logs (species, counts, locations, actions taken) for each grow.

4. **Biological controls:**
   - Predatory mites (Phytoseiulus persimilis, Neoseiulus californicus) for mites; predatory insects (lacewings, lady beetles) for aphids; entomopathogenic fungi (Beauveria bassiana) for thrips and whiteflies.
   - Beneficial bacteria (Bacillus subtilis) and fungi (Trichoderma harzianum) to outcompete pathogens and induce plant defences.
   - Use of mycorrhizae and beneficial soil microbes to enhance root health and nutrient uptake.

5. **Mechanical & physical controls:**
   - Sticky traps, insect netting and screens on air intakes.
   - Manual removal of heavily infested leaves; using vacuum devices; pruning infected parts.

6. **Chemical controls & regulatory compliance:**
   - Distinguish between organic and synthetic pesticides.  Emphasise products approved for cannabis or edible crops.  Discuss modes of action (contact vs systemic) and importance of rotating chemicals to avoid resistance.
   - Follow labelled rates, pre‑harvest intervals and safety measures (PPE, re‑entry intervals).
   - Understand Nevada requirements for pesticide disclosure and warnings when selling products【529056685724377†L269-L290】.

7. **Integrated Pest Management (IPM) plan:**
   - Combining prevention, monitoring and control methods; emphasising least toxic options first.
   - Setting action thresholds, selecting appropriate treatments and evaluating efficacy.
   - Continual improvement through data analysis and adjusting strategies.

### Assessments

- **Pest identification quiz:** Show micrographs or photos of various pests and diseases; students identify them and list symptoms.
- **IPM plan assignment:** Develop a season-long IPM plan for a 24-plant garden, including monitoring schedule, cultural practices and choice of biological/chemical controls.
- **Case study:** Evaluate a powdery mildew outbreak scenario and describe immediate and long-term solutions.
- **Label reading exercise:** Given pesticide labels, identify active ingredients, signal words, re-entry intervals and compatibility with cannabis cultivation.

## Module 5 – Cultivation Techniques

### Learning objectives

- Apply propagation methods (seeds, cloning, tissue culture) to produce vigorous plants.
- Employ training and pruning techniques to maximise canopy efficiency and yield.
- Manage flowering, harvest and post-harvest processes to preserve potency and quality.
- Evaluate advanced cultivation systems such as vertical farming and hydroponics.

### Instructional content

1. **Propagation:**
   - **Seeds:** Germination techniques (paper towel method, direct sowing); importance of seed viability and storage; feminised vs regular seeds; genetic stability.
   - **Clones:** Selecting healthy mother plants; taking cuttings at appropriate nodes; using rooting hormones; maintaining high humidity with domes; transplanting rooted clones into media.
   - **Tissue culture (advanced):** Introduction to micropropagation; sterilisation, explant selection, media preparation, callus formation; advantages for disease-free propagation and strain preservation.

2. **Transplanting & potting:**
   - Choosing container sizes and materials (fabric pots, plastic, air pots).
   - Transplanting timing based on root development; techniques to avoid root damage; hardening off clones.
   - Importance of potting mix composition and drainage.

3. **Training & pruning techniques:**
   - **Topping and FIMming:** Encouraging bushier growth and multiple colas.
   - **Low stress training (LST):** Bending and securing branches to create an even canopy; using tie-downs and plant clips.
   - **High stress training (HST):** Super cropping (stem bending), pruning and lollipopping (removing lower growth to concentrate energy on top colas).
   - **Screen of Green (SCROG):** Installing a trellis net to train branches horizontally; benefits for even light distribution.
   - **Sea of Green (SOG):** Growing many small plants in small containers; early flowering for rapid turnover; considerations for plant counts under legal limits.
   - **Defoliation:** Removing excess leaves to improve light penetration and airflow; timing (late veg, early flower) to minimise stress.

4. **Flowering management:**
   - Stretch management: supporting branches, using trellises or yo-yos; controlling vegetative growth before flip.
   - Adjusting feeding regimens: reducing nitrogen and increasing phosphorus and potassium during bloom.
   - Monitoring trichome maturity with jeweller’s loupe; differences between clear, cloudy and amber trichomes.

5. **Harvest & post-harvest:**
   - Determining harvest time: pistil colour, trichome colour, bud density, breeder recommendations.
   - **Flushing:** Use of plain water or minimal nutrients 1–2 weeks before harvest to remove excess salts.
   - **Trimming:** Wet vs dry trimming; using shears or automated machines; handling buds gently to preserve trichomes.
   - **Drying:** Ideal conditions (60 °F / 15 °C, 50–60 % RH); using drying racks or hang lines; importance of slow drying to prevent chlorophyll retention.
   - **Curing:** Placing dry buds in airtight containers (glass jars); burping jars to release moisture; using humidity packs to maintain 55–62 % RH; curing for 2–6 weeks for flavour and smoothness.
   - **Packaging & storage:** Proper labeling; child-resistant containers; UV protection; long-term storage considerations (cool, dark, sealed environment).

6. **Advanced cultivation systems:**
   - **Vertical farming:** Multi-tier racking, LED bars, automated irrigation; benefits for space efficiency; mechanical lifts for access; potential issues with microclimates and pest transfer.
   - **Hydroponics & aeroponics:** Recirculating vs drain-to-waste systems; benefits and drawbacks; importance of sterile conditions.
   - **Living soil & organic cultivation:** Building a super soil with compost, worm castings, biochar; encouraging microbial diversity; using cover crops; minimal or no bottled nutrients.
   - **CO₂ enrichment and high-density planting:** Combining supplemental CO₂ with high light and high nutrient delivery for maximum yield; emphasise safety and equipment.

### Assessments

- **Practical demonstration:** Students perform topping, LST and lollipopping on demonstration plants.  Instructors evaluate technique and plant recovery.
- **Training plan:** Draft a training schedule for a ten-week crop, including when to top, prune and defoliate.
- **Harvest readiness evaluation:** Students inspect sample buds under magnification and determine harvest time, justifying their decisions.
- **Post-harvest essay:** Describe proper drying, curing and storage processes and how each step affects cannabinoid and terpene preservation.
- **System comparison chart:** Compare vertical farming, soil and hydroponic systems for yield, cost, complexity and labour requirements.

## Module 6 – Compliance, Safety & Ethics

### Learning objectives

- Apply Nevada’s legal framework for home cultivation (age limits, plant counts, enclosure requirements)【347000102993283†L349-L364】【98296420791439†L268-L277】.
- Understand the licensing categories and requirements for opening and operating a cannabis establishment in Nevada, including capital, location restrictions and security【529056685724377†L135-L160】.
- Describe Metrc seed-to-sale tracking requirements and implement accurate record-keeping【749967051204703†L523-L575】.
- Develop standard operating procedures (SOPs) that meet regulatory, safety and ethical standards.
- Recognise ethical considerations in cannabis cultivation and sales (age verification, consumer education, pesticide disclosure and environmental sustainability).

### Instructional content

1. **Nevada home cultivation rules:**
   - Only adults 21 + are allowed to grow cannabis at home; they may cultivate up to six plants per person with a maximum of 12 plants per household【347000102993283†L349-L364】【98296420791439†L268-L277】.
   - Home growers must live more than 25 miles from a licensed retail store and must grow plants in an enclosed area equipped with a lock or security device; plants cannot be visible from a public place【347000102993283†L349-L364】.
   - The grower must either own the property or have permission from the property owner to cultivate【347000102993283†L349-L364】.  Condominiums and HOAs may impose additional restrictions【708018878413687†L149-L152】.

2. **Licensed establishment compliance:**
   - **License types:** Nevada issues multiple licenses, including cultivation, production, retail, distribution and testing【749967051204703†L539-L556】.  Cultivation facilities grow, harvest and package cannabis; production facilities manufacture edibles and concentrates; dispensaries sell directly to consumers; distributors transport cannabis between establishments; testing labs ensure product safety.
   - **Establishment requirements:** To open a cannabis business, applicants must control at least $250,000 in liquid assets, have no disqualifying felony convictions, pay application and licensing fees, obtain landlord permission, and comply with local zoning.  Facilities must be located at least 1,000 feet from schools and 300 feet from community facilities; signage must be discreet; entrances must meet fire code; and professional interiors must resemble medical offices【529056685724377†L135-L160】.
   - **Security & surveillance:** Licensed facilities must have 24/7 video surveillance, restricted access areas, alarm systems and secure storage; entry logs must be maintained; employees must wear ID badges.
   - **Seed-to-sale tracking (Metrc):** Each plant and package receives a unique RFID tag; all movements (planting, harvesting, drying, packaging, transfers and sales) are recorded in the Metrc system; employees must complete Metrc training and maintain accurate, timely records【749967051204703†L523-L575】【749967051204703†L560-L599】.  Regular audits and reconciliations ensure data integrity【749967051204703†L577-L583】; failure to comply may result in fines or license suspension【749967051204703†L523-L575】.
   - **Inventory management:** Use RFID tags to track inventory; maintain separate storage for different product categories; implement regular stock counts; maintain detailed logs (quantity, date, time, employee ID, license number) for each sale【749967051204703†L523-L575】【529056685724377†L247-L256】.
   - **Pesticide disclosure & labelling:** Facilities must provide notifications about pesticides used during production and include warnings regarding health risks; labels must state THC content, batch number, harvest date, and warnings about intoxication and child safety【529056685724377†L269-L290】.
   - **Health & safety requirements:** Provide access to handwashing sinks and proper sanitation; maintain equipment cleanliness; follow NCCR regulations for edible production facilities (e.g., equipment lists, floor plans, proper cooling and refrigeration, and water supply)【347491545219015†L26-L122】.

3. **Worker safety & PPE:**
   - Use gloves, masks and eye protection when handling nutrients, pesticides and solvents.
   - Educate staff about electrical hazards and safe operation of equipment (ballasts, high-powered LEDs, CO₂ systems).  Use ground-fault circuit interrupters (GFCIs) in wet areas.
   - Provide training on chemical storage, emergency procedures and first aid.

4. **Ethics & responsible conduct:**
   - **Age verification:** Retail agents must verify the age of consumers using government-issued ID and approved scanners【529056685724377†L231-L236】.
   - **Consumer education:** Distribute educational materials regarding safe consumption, proper dosing and potential health risks【529056685724377†L240-L296】.
   - **Advertising restrictions:** Avoid targeting minors; maintain professional signage and marketing materials.
   - **Environmental sustainability:** Adopt best management practices to reduce energy consumption, reuse water, recycle waste and choose sustainable packaging.
   - **Data privacy:** Keep client information confidential; secure digital systems; comply with data protection laws.

5. **Record-keeping & SOPs:**
   - Maintain detailed logs of plant counts, feedings, pesticide applications, environmental parameters and pest monitoring; use digital systems that integrate with Metrc when required.
   - Develop written SOPs covering every aspect of operations—from propagation to harvest—and update them regularly.
   - Conduct internal audits to ensure adherence to SOPs and legal requirements.

### Assessments

- **Compliance exam:** Written exam covering plant limits, enclosure requirements, licensing categories, Metrc rules and pesticide disclosure.  Scenario-based questions test ability to apply regulations.
- **SOP development project:** Students draft a standard operating procedure for a chosen process (e.g., nutrient mixing, pest monitoring or harvest handling) incorporating legal and safety requirements.
- **Inspection role-play:** Simulate a regulatory inspection at a facility; students play the role of facility manager and respond to inspector questions about record keeping, security and compliance.
- **Ethics reflection:** Write a short essay on how ethical conduct and responsible marketing contribute to consumer safety and industry credibility.

## Module 7 – Customer Service & Business Skills

### Learning objectives

- Communicate effectively and professionally with clients and team members.
- Manage time and resources efficiently to serve multiple clients and maintain high standards.
- Produce clear, concise reports and records using digital tools.
- Understand the fundamentals of sales, client retention and brand representation.

### Instructional content

1. **Professional communication:**
   - Active listening and empathy; tailoring explanations to client knowledge level; delivering constructive feedback.
   - Handling difficult conversations (e.g., explaining why certain practices are illegal or unsafe); maintaining professionalism under stress.
   - Communicating with team members using digital messaging tools and shared task lists; ensuring everyone is aligned.

2. **Scheduling & time management:**
   - Using the MGC portal to schedule visits, set reminders and allocate time efficiently.  Balancing in-person and virtual appointments.
   - Prioritisation techniques (e.g., Eisenhower matrix) to handle urgent vs important tasks; creating daily and weekly plans.

3. **Reporting & documentation:**
   - Writing plant health reports: summarising observations, noting environmental data, recommending treatments and outlining action steps.
   - Capturing high-quality photographs; organising before/after images; using digital forms to record nutrient and IPM activities.
   - Complying with legal requirements for record retention.

4. **Sales & client retention:**
   - Identifying client needs and recommending suitable subscription plans or equipment upgrades without overselling.
   - Building trust through consistent results and honest communication; encouraging referrals; handling cancellations and churn.
   - Basic financial literacy: understanding margins, cost of labour, equipment costs, revenue streams and how service tiers contribute to company profitability.

5. **Brand representation & confidentiality:**
   - Following MGC’s brand identity guidelines: using approved tone, visuals and names; never revealing owner identity or personal information.
   - Maintaining confidentiality of client data and grow locations.
   - Respecting clients’ privacy and property; obtaining consent before taking photos or adjusting equipment.

### Assessments

- **Role-play exercise:** Students simulate a remote consultation with a new client, addressing the client’s concerns, explaining service tiers and offering an appropriate plan.
- **Report writing assignment:** Generate a full report for a hypothetical client after a diagnostic visit, including environmental readings, nutrient recommendations and pest observations.
- **Time management plan:** Create a weekly schedule for a technician servicing 20 clients, balancing travel, remote support and administrative tasks.
- **Sales presentation:** Prepare a short presentation promoting a grow setup package and highlight the value of an upgraded subscription.

## Module 8 – Practicum & Assessment

### Learning objectives

- Apply theoretical knowledge in real-world scenarios under instructor supervision.
- Demonstrate competence in diagnostics, maintenance, training, record keeping and client interaction.
- Develop a comprehensive cultivation plan that integrates best practices, compliance and sustainability.

### Instructional content

1. **Fieldwork:**
   - Apprentices shadow certified technicians during home visits and virtual consultations.  They assist with measuring temperature, humidity and EC; mixing nutrient solutions; identifying pests and performing IPM treatments; and executing training and pruning techniques.
   - Apprentices practise safe use of equipment (pH/EC meters, microscopes, sprayers, CO₂ systems); practice proper sanitation and waste disposal.
   - Trainees learn to communicate with clients respectfully, answer questions and document observations in the MGC portal.

2. **Project assignments:**
   - **Home grow plan:** Each student designs a plan for a six-plant home operation (targeting Nevada compliance) that includes grow space layout, environmental controls, feeding schedule, IPM plan and harvest timeline.  The plan must address enclosure security and property permission requirements【347000102993283†L349-L364】.
   - **Commercial cultivation plan:** For advanced certification, students design a 50-light indoor cultivation plan that complies with licensed facility requirements (e.g., Metrc tracking, security protocols, inventory control, proper location distances【529056685724377†L135-L160】).  The plan should include HVAC sizing, lighting layout, redundancy, SOPs, and compliance measures.

3. **Final examination:**
   - **Written exam:** 50–75 questions covering all modules (multiple choice, short answer and scenario-based questions).
   - **Practical exam:** Each candidate performs key tasks (top and train plants, measure and adjust pH/EC, calibrate environmental sensors, identify and treat pests, complete a Metrc entry) while being observed by an instructor.
   - **Presentation:** Students present their cultivation plan to a panel (instructors and industry professionals), answering questions and receiving feedback.

### Assessments

- **Continuous assessment:** Instructors evaluate trainees’ performance during fieldwork, noting adherence to SOPs, quality of work and communication skills.
- **Final project:** Graded on thoroughness, accuracy, regulatory compliance, environmental sustainability and presentation skills.
- **Written & practical exams:** Minimum passing score (e.g., 80 %) required for certification.  Students must successfully complete both the written test and the practicum.
- **Post-graduation evaluation:** After certification, technicians complete at least 100 hours of supervised fieldwork and submit three client reports for review before becoming fully independent.

## Optional Specialist Modules (Advanced)

For participants aiming to become advanced cultivation specialists capable of working in large licensed facilities, optional modules provide deeper knowledge and specialised skills.

### Specialist Module A – Facility Design & Automation

- Facility layout planning (perpetual harvest design, vegetative and flower room segmentation, mothers and clone rooms).
- Advanced HVAC design (chilled water systems vs mini splits, variable refrigerant flow (VRF) systems) and redundancy.
- Lighting distribution for multi-tier racks and rolling benches; calculating heat loads.
- Control system integration (PLC or BMS) for environmental parameters, irrigation and fertigation.
- Electrical load calculations; compliance with fire codes; backup power systems.
- Workflow and labour efficiency analysis; designing ergonomic workstations.

### Specialist Module B – Quality Control & Testing

- Sampling and sending material to independent labs; understanding potency, terpene and contaminant analyses.
- Interpreting lab results; adjusting cultivation practices based on test outcomes.
- Standard operating procedures for sampling, packaging and chain of custody.
- Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) and Hazard Analysis Critical Control Point (HACCP) principles applied to cannabis.

### Specialist Module C – Breeding & Genetics

- Mendelian genetics fundamentals; phenotype vs genotype; heterosis and genetic drift.
- Breeding methods: open pollination, selfing, backcrossing, stabilising desirable traits.
- Selecting and evaluating breeding stock; phenohunting; record keeping.
- Intellectual property considerations: plant patents, trademarks and licensing.

### Specialist Module D – Sustainability & Resource Management

- Energy auditing and efficiency upgrades (LED retrofits, heat recovery, solar integration).
- Water conservation methods: drip irrigation, recapture and reuse, condensate recovery from HVAC.
- Waste management: composting plant waste; recycling soil and coco; safe disposal of hazardous waste.
- Carbon footprint reduction strategies: carbon offsets, renewable power, regenerative cultivation practices.
- Certification programs (LEED, Growsafe) and government incentives for sustainable cultivation.
